what is the arvelie date system?

Posted on 00Y10

The Arvelie calendar is a compact alternative dating system created by Devine Lu Linvega. For a full technical description of how it works (including the exact mapping, leap rules, and examples), see the original page on the XXIIVV wiki.

Here are a few examples (using 2025 is the base year):

  • January 1, 2025 -> 00A00
  • April 1, 2025 -> 00G06
  • October 1, 2025 -> 00T07
  • December 31, 2025 -> 00+00
  • March 15, 2027 -> 02F03

why i like it

My favorite part of Arvelie is how it turns time into something personal and relative instead of absolute and historical. Most calendars pin everything to an ancient event, such as AD (Anno Domini) or BC (Before Christ). Arvelie lets you start the clock at whatever matters to you, like the day a website began (this is how I use it).

That relativity feels freeing. Time becomes about “how long has this thing existed” rather than “what year is it in the big shared timeline.” It is the same way we naturally think about age, relationships, or how long we have been working on something. It becomes more personal, something perfect for this website.

The structure itself is elegant: 26 uniform fortnights. No short months, no weird 28/29/30/31 rules. Dates sort easily as plain strings, are short enough to type quickly, and look a little futuristic without being unreadable.

Arvelie also accounts for the extra days that don’t fit neatly into its 26x14 structure. After the last month (Z), there is a special Year Day, written as +00. This day stands outside of any month or week and represents the final day of the year. So 2025-12-31 becomes 00+00.

On leap years, an additional Leap Day is inserted just before the Year Day. This is written as +01. Like the Year Day, it does not belong to any month and exists purely to keep the calendar aligned with the solar year. So 2028-12-31 becomes 03+01.

how i use it on this site

This site launched in 2025, so I set 2025 as year 00. Every date on the site starts with 00 this year (2025), rolling to 01 next year (2026), and so on. It ties the calendar directly to the project’s origin.

To implement it, I built a Hugo partial date-arvelie.html that automatically converts post dates in my html templates, plus a shortcode arvelie.html (optionally with a date parameter) for use in Markdown.

The logic was heavily inspired by the excellent CLI tool from Tillman Jex.

This date system is a small tweak, but it makes this whole site feel a bit more intentional and mine.
