what is the arvelie date system?

Posted on 00Y10

The Arvelie calendar is a compact alternative dating system created by Devine Lu Linvega.

It is written in YYMDD format. The year starts at 0 from a certain base year, usually the start of a journal. There are 26 months, each denoted by a letter of the alphabet (A-Z), and each month contains 2 weeks of 7 days each, so 14 in total.

Here are a few examples (using 2025 as the base year):

  • January 1, 2025 -> 00A00
  • April 1, 2025 -> 00G06
  • October 5, 2025 -> 00T11
  • December 31, 2025 -> 00+00
  • March 15, 2027 -> 02F03

why i like it

My favorite part of Arvelie is how it turns time into something personal and relative instead of absolute. Most calendars pin everything to an ancient event, usually AD (Anno Domini) or BC (Before Christ). Arvelie lets you start the clock at whatever matters to you, like the day a website was created (like mine!).

That relativity is very satisfying to me. Time becomes about “how long has this thing existed” rather than “what year is it in the big shared timeline.” It is the same way we naturally think about age (your birth year is your year 0, and you count up from there), relationships (anniversary years counted up), or how long we have been working on something. It becomes more personal, something perfect for this website.

The structure itself is elegant: 26 uniform fortnights. No short months, no weird 28/29/30/31 rules. Dates sort easily as plain strings, are short enough to type quickly, and look a little futuristic without being unreadable.

Arvelie also accounts for the extra days that don’t fit neatly into its 26x14 structure. After the last month (Z), there is a special Year Day, written as +00. This day stands outside of any month or week and represents the final day of the year. So 2025-12-31 becomes 00+00.

On leap years, the Year Day is followed by a Leap Day. This is written as +01. Like the Year Day, it does not belong to any month and exists purely to keep the calendar aligned with the solar year. So on a leap year like 2028, 2028-12-30 becomes 03+00 (the Year Day) and 2028-12-31 becomes 03+01 (the Leap Day).

how i use it on this site

This site launched in 2025, so I set 2025 as year 00. Every date on the site starts with 00 this year (2025), rolling to 01 next year (2026), and so on. It ties the calendar directly to the project’s origin.

To implement it, I built a Hugo partial date-arvelie.html that automatically converts post dates in my html templates, plus a shortcode arvelie.html (optionally with a date parameter) for use in Markdown. (The examples above were made using said shortcode.)

The logic was heavily inspired by the excellent CLI tool from Tillman Jex.

final thoughts

This date system is a small tweak, but it makes this whole site feel a bit more intentional and mine. I plan to keep using Arvelie here for the long run, letting the calendar grow alongside the site itself. As the years tick up from zero, the dates will not just mark when something was written, but how far this little project has come.
