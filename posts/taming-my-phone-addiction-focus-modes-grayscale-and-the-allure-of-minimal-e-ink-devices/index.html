taming my phone addiction: focus modes, grayscale, and the allure of minimal e-ink devices

Posted on 01A03

In an era where smartphones are designed to capture our attention, I’ve been grappling with my own device dependency. On my iPhone 16 Pro, my average daily screen time hovers around 3 hours. Not too high, but I still feel it pulling me away from real-life priorities. The biggest culprits? Endless YouTube rabbit holes, constant texting, and aimless web surfing. These distractions have hit hardest during my practice sessions, where I need undivided focus but often find myself checking notifications mid-routine. It’s led to fragmented concentration, less productive practice sessions, and a nagging sense that my phone can be more of a hindrance than a help.

To combat this, I’ve implemented some built-in iOS features to make my iPhone less addictive. While these tweaks aren’t a complete cure, they’ve helped reclaim some mental space. But lately, I’ve been eyeing hardware alternatives, specifically minimal devices with e-ink displays that promise a calmer, less stimulating experience. I absolutely love the e-ink tech from my Kindle; it feels gentle on the eyes and evokes a sense of tranquility that’s worlds apart from the vibrant, notification-pinging glow of my iPhone. Below, I’ll share my current setup and why devices like the Boox Palma (and a few others) are calling my name.

My iPhone Tweaks: Focus Modes and Grayscale

Apple’s Focus Modes have been a game-changer for tailoring my phone to specific activities. I have three main ones: Practice, School, and Workout. Each allows only essential apps on a one-page setup and limits notifications, silencing the rest. Here’s a breakdown:

Practice Focus

This is for my dedicated practice time.

Apps on the home screen:

  • Andante (my practice tracker)
  • TE Tuner (for tuning my instruments)
  • Voice Memos (to record myself)
  • Apple Music (for listening to recordings)

Notifications are limited to Calendar reminders, so no social media or random pings to derail me.

This focus mode is triggered manually, through the shortcut at the top of each home page.

School Focus

This focus mode keeps me productive during classes or study sessions.

Apps on the home screen:

  • Google Drive (to access school files)
  • Infinite Campus (for checking grades)
  • Canvas (for accessing assignments)
  • Gmail (for my school email)
  • Apple Music (for my study playlist)

Widgets on the home screen:

  • Bell Schedule (so I’m not late to class)
  • Homework (see my homework todo list)
  • Stations (put on a Music station)

Notifications come through for the aforementioned apps, so I can stay on top of assignments without extras.

This focus mode is triggered automatically by location when I am at my school, or manually through the shortcut at the top of each home page.

Workout Focus

This focus mode is for my workouts, whether lifting at the gym or running.

Apps on the home screen:

  • Fit19 (the gym I frequent)
  • Apple Fitness (to see my activity)
  • Podcasts
  • Notes (to see my workout plans)
  • Music
  • Strava (to track my workouts)

This focus mode is triggered automatically when an Apple Workout is started.

Color Filters

Another key feature I use is the grayscale color filter, which I keep on most of the time, and is mapped to my Action Button. Accessed via Accessibility settings, it turns the entire screen black & white, making colorful apps like YouTube or social media far less appealing. Everything feels more “boring,” which is exactly the point: it discourages mindless scrolling. I toggle it off only when editing photos or needing accurate colors, but otherwise, it is my default. Combined with Focus Modes, my screen time has dipped, and I feel more present, especially during workouts and practice.

These software fixes are effective to a point, but they still live on a powerful, temptation-filled device. That’s why I’m drawn to minimal hardware alternatives that inherently limit distractions through design.

The Appeal of Minimal E-Ink Devices

While my iPhone is a powerhouse, its high-refresh OLED screen and app ecosystem make it addictive by nature. Enter minimal e-ink devices: phone-sized gadgets with paper-like displays that refresh slowly, last days or weeks on a charge, and prioritize focus over flash. They’re not full smartphone replacements (most lack native calling), but they handle essentials like messaging via apps, reading, and notes, without the dopamine hits. I love how e-ink feels and looks; it’s soothing, eye-friendly, and transports me to a different mindset, much like flipping pages in a book.

The Boox Palma 2 Pro: My Top Contender

The Boox Palma series, especially the Palma 2 Pro, is the one I’m looking at the most. Its phone-like size makes it portable, but the e-ink display changes everything. The latest version features a color e-ink screen (Kaleido 3), which would make it more similar to a phone, so I might consider a different version that is only in black and white.

Running Android 15 with full Play Store access, it can function almost like a phone: install WhatsApp, Signal, or another app for messaging over Wi-Fi or 5G (with a hybrid SIM slot for data).

note
With AirMessage, one can use their Apple iMessages on any device, and even on the web!

It has 8GB RAM, 128GB storage (expandable), a camera for quick scans, and built-in speakers for podcasts or music. Battery life is stellar, lasting days or weeks thanks to e-ink’s low power draw. The slower refresh rate (albeit very impressive for an e-ink display) naturally deters addictive habits like endless scrolling or gaming. Apps work, but they’re not as engaging.

Drawbacks? Since it is an e-ink screen, dynamic content like YouTube can have refresh artifacts, but that is expected. The camera is basic, but has a nice vintage aesthetic that I enjoy. Still, for my needs such as reading ebooks, note-taking with the stylus, podcasts and music during workouts, and minimal communication, it feels ideal. It’d complement my iPhone: use the Palma for most tasks and the iPhone for high-res media.

Wrapping Up: A Step Toward Mindful Tech

My iPhone tweaks have helped curb distractions, boosting focus during practice, school, and workouts. But for true minimalism, I’m leaning toward an e-ink companion like the Boox Palma 2 Pro. Its paper-like display and deliberate pace could transform how I interact with tech, making it a tool rather than a time-sink. If you’re feeling phone-fatigued, start with software changes like Focus Modes, then consider going minimal. I’ll update this post if I make the switch!
